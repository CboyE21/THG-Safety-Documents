PPE
Talk #1 Respiratory Protection for Drywall Dust PPE
Overview

Drywall sanding produces fine silica and gypsum dust that can cause serious lung disease over time. Proper respiratory protection is essential every time you sand.

Key Points
  • Always wear at minimum an N95 respirator when sanding drywall or joint compound.
  • Fit-test your respirator — a loose mask provides little protection.
  • Disposable respirators must be discarded when breathing becomes difficult or the mask is damaged.
  • If sanding in a confined or poorly ventilated space, upgrade to a half-face respirator with P100 filters.
  • Never eat, drink, or smoke in dusty work areas — wash your hands and face before breaks.

Talk #2 Eye Protection on the Job PPE
Overview

Eye injuries are among the most common construction injuries and are almost entirely preventable with proper PPE.

Key Points
  • Safety glasses must be worn when cutting, sanding, spraying, or mixing materials.
  • Use goggles or a face shield when spraying paint, texture, or applying chemicals.
  • Prescription glasses alone do not meet ANSI Z87.1 safety standards.
  • Inspect safety glasses daily for scratches or cracks that reduce visibility.
  • Know the location of the eyewash station or emergency water source on site.
Discussion Question

Talk #3 Glove Selection for Painting & Insulation Work PPE
Overview

Different tasks require different gloves. Using the wrong glove — or no glove — can result in chemical burns, cuts, or skin sensitization.

Key Points
  • Use nitrile gloves when handling paints, solvents, primers, or adhesives.
  • Wear cut-resistant gloves when cutting drywall, mesh tape, or insulation batts.
  • Heavy-duty work gloves protect against abrasion when handling metal studs or fiber insulation.
  • Never use latex gloves near solvents — they offer minimal chemical resistance.
  • Replace gloves immediately if they are torn or contaminated on the inside.

Talk #4 Safe Handling & Lifting of Drywall Sheets Drywall
Overview

A single 4x8 sheet of 5/8" drywall weighs over 70 lbs. Back injuries from improper lifting are one of the top causes of lost work time in our trade.

Key Points
  • Always lift with your legs, not your back — bend at the knees and keep your back straight.
  • Use a drywall cart or panel lift for moving multiple sheets.
  • Two-person lifts are required for sheets 4x12 and larger.
  • Panel lifts must be inspected before use — check wheels, clamps, and the lift mechanism.
  • Carry sheets on edge, not flat, to reduce stress on the sheet and your body.
  • Clear the path before moving large panels — trip hazards are especially dangerous when vision is obstructed.
Discussion Question

Talk #5 Cutting Drywall Safely Drywall
Overview

Drywall knives and routers are sharp tools used constantly on our jobs. Cuts and lacerations are preventable with the right habits.

Key Points
  • Always cut away from your body and keep fingers clear of the cut line.
  • Retract utility knife blades when not in use — never leave an exposed blade on a surface.
  • Change blades regularly — dull blades require more force and are more likely to slip.
  • Use a straightedge or T-square to guide cuts instead of freehand cutting.
  • When using a drywall router, secure the panel before cutting and keep cords clear of the cutting path.
Discussion Question
Are all cutting tools in good condition today? Have blades been changed recently?
Talk #6 Silica Dust Awareness Drywall
Overview

Silica is found in drywall compound and can cause silicosis — a permanent, potentially fatal lung disease. OSHA has strict standards for silica exposure.

Key Points
  • Use wet methods or vacuum-equipped tools when sanding where feasible.
  • Wear an N95 or higher respirator any time dry sanding is performed.
  • Avoid blowing dust with compressed air — this creates high airborne silica concentrations.
  • Ventilate work areas and use plastic sheeting to contain dust to the work zone.
  • Clean up with a wet mop or HEPA vacuum — never dry sweep silica-containing dust.

Painting
Talk #7 Paint & Solvent Safety Painting
Overview

Paints, primers, and solvents contain chemicals that can be harmful if inhaled, ingested, or absorbed through the skin. Always review the SDS before using a new product.

Key Points
  • Know where the SDS binder is kept on every job site.
  • Check the SDS for the correct PPE — respirator type, glove material, and eye protection.
  • Store flammable solvents in approved containers away from ignition sources.
  • Never mix paint products unless specifically directed — chemical reactions can produce toxic fumes.
  • Dispose of solvent-soaked rags in an approved metal safety can to prevent spontaneous combustion.
  • Report any symptoms of dizziness, headache, or nausea when working with solvents immediately.
Discussion Question
Where is the SDS book on this site? Has everyone reviewed the SDS for today's products?
Talk #8 Spray Painting Safety Painting
Overview

Airless sprayers operate at extremely high pressure and can inject paint through skin, causing serious injury. Spray painting also creates heavy airborne mist and requires full PPE.

Key Points
  • Never point an airless sprayer at any person — even without the tip installed, pressure can injure.
  • Always engage the safety lock when not actively spraying.
  • Wear a supplied-air respirator or N95 minimum when spraying in enclosed areas.
  • Wear chemical splash goggles — not just safety glasses — when spraying.
  • Ground the sprayer properly to prevent static buildup when spraying flammable coatings.
  • Ensure adequate ventilation — open windows, run exhaust fans, or use blowers.
Discussion Question

Talk #9 Ladder Safety for Painters Painting
Overview

Painters spend a significant part of their workday on ladders. Falls from ladders are one of the leading causes of fatalities in construction.

Key Points
  • Inspect every ladder before use — look for cracks, bent rungs, and missing feet.
  • Set extension ladders at a 4:1 angle — 1 foot out for every 4 feet of height.
  • Always maintain three points of contact when climbing.
  • Never carry a paint bucket or sprayer with both hands while climbing — use a hook or bucket hanger.
  • Do not stand on the top two rungs of a stepladder or the top three rungs of an extension ladder.
  • Secure the top and bottom of extension ladders on slippery or uneven surfaces.
Discussion Question
Are all ladders on site inspected and in good condition? Are they the correct height for today's tasks?
Insulation
Talk #10 Fiberglass Insulation Safety Insulation
Overview

Fiberglass insulation contains tiny glass fibers that can irritate skin, eyes, and the respiratory tract. Proper PPE makes handling insulation comfortable and safe.

Key Points
  • Wear long sleeves and long pants — skin exposure causes itching and irritation.
  • Wear safety glasses or goggles to prevent fiber contact with eyes.
  • Wear an N95 respirator when handling or cutting fiberglass batts.
  • Wash exposed skin with cool water after contact — hot water opens pores and traps fibers.
  • Change and wash work clothing before leaving the job site to avoid bringing fibers home.

Talk #11 Spray Foam Insulation Hazards Insulation
Overview

Two-component spray polyurethane foam contains isocyanates — chemicals that can cause permanent respiratory sensitization and asthma. Once sensitized, re-exposure can be life-threatening.

Key Points
  • Only trained workers should apply two-component spray foam.
  • A supplied-air respirator (SAR) is required during application — not just an N95.
  • The work area must be evacuated and ventilated during and after application.
  • Wear full chemical-resistant coveralls, gloves, and goggles when handling components.
  • Skin contact with uncured foam can cause chemical burns — wash off immediately.

Fall Protection
Talk #12 Scaffold Safety Fall Protection
Overview

Scaffolding is used frequently for ceiling work, high walls, and exterior applications. Scaffold-related falls are fatal. Never take shortcuts with scaffold safety.

Key Points
  • Only competent persons may erect, move, or dismantle scaffolding.
  • Inspect the scaffold at the start of each shift — look for missing guardrails, cross-braces, and damaged planks.
  • All scaffold platforms must be fully planked — no gaps where a worker or tool can fall through.
  • Guardrails must be in place on all open sides and ends above 10 feet.
  • Never overload scaffolding — know the rated capacity and stay within it.
  • Keep the scaffold clear of debris, tools, and cords that could cause a trip.

Talk #13 Floor & Wall Opening Hazards Fall Protection
Overview

Open holes in floors and walls are common during construction. An unguarded opening is an immediate fall hazard that must be addressed before work begins.

Key Points
  • All floor openings must be covered or guarded before work begins in the area.
  • Floor hole covers must be secured so they cannot be accidentally displaced.
  • Label all floor hole covers clearly: "HOLE — DO NOT REMOVE".
  • Wall openings above 6 feet from the lower level require guardrails or a safety net.
  • If you remove a cover or guardrail to do work, replace it immediately when finished.

Talk #14 Electrical Safety & GFCI Protection Tools & Equipment
Overview

Electrocution is one of the top four causes of construction fatalities. Temporary power on construction sites must always be protected by GFCI devices.

Key Points
  • All temporary power on construction sites must be protected by a Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI).
  • Test GFCI devices at the start of each day before use.
  • Never use damaged extension cords — frayed wires and exposed conductors are immediately hazardous.
  • Keep cords out of water, away from sharp edges, and off the floor where they can be damaged by traffic.
  • Never remove the ground prong from a three-prong plug.
  • If a GFCI trips repeatedly, there is a fault in the circuit — do not reset and ignore it.
Discussion Question
Have the GFCIs been tested today? Are all extension cords in good condition?

Talk #15 Heat Illness Prevention Health & Wellness
Overview

Working in hot environments — including poorly ventilated buildings in summer — puts workers at risk for heat cramps, heat exhaustion, and life-threatening heat stroke.

Key Points
  • Drink water frequently — at least one cup every 20 minutes in hot conditions.
  • Take breaks in cool or shaded areas — especially in the first few days of hot weather.
  • Know the signs of heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache.
  • Heat stroke is a medical emergency — hot dry skin, confusion, or loss of consciousness requires a 911 call immediately.
  • Watch out for co-workers — look for signs of heat illness in others and speak up.
  • New workers and those returning from time off need 7–14 days to acclimatize to heat.
